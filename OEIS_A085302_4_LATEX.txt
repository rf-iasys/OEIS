\section*{Introduction}

In this note, we explore an unexpected connection between an inequality involving factorials and primorials, and two OEIS sequences:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A085301:} Number of factorials between consecutive primorials.
    \item \textbf{A085302:} Partial sum of A085301, i.e., the total number of factorials below the $n$-th primorial.
\end{itemize}

We will describe the sequences, define the inequality, and highlight the surprising alignment.

\section*{Definitions}

\subsection*{Factorials and Primorials}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{factorial} of $n$ is
    \[
        n! = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdots n.
    \]
    \item The \textbf{primorial} of $n$ is
    \[
        n\# = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p_i,
    \]
    where $p_i$ is the $i$-th prime.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{OEIS Sequences}

\textbf{A085301:} Number of factorials between consecutive primorials:
\[
    a(n) = \#\{ k \mid q(n) \le k! \le q(n+1) \}, \quad q(n) = n\#.
\]

\textbf{A085302:} Cumulative sum of A085301:
\[
    A085302(n) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} A085301(j).
\]
Equivalently, $A085302(n)-1$ counts the number of factorials strictly less than $n\#$.

\section*{The Factorial-Primorial Inequality}

Define a sequence of integers $k$ for each $n$ by solving the inequality:
\[
    n! < n\# + k\#,
\]
and let $k = k(n)$ be the \emph{smallest integer} satisfying this inequality.

Observations from computational experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The sequence of $k(n)$ values coincides with \emph{primes}.
    \item The sequence of $n$ where $k(n)$ changes matches exactly the indices of OEIS A085302.
\end{enumerate}

Thus, although the base calculation is completely different from the A085301/A085302 definition, the resulting sequence is identical.

\section*{Numerical Evidence}

\begin{itemize}
    \item First 10 elements of the $n$ sequence from the inequality:
    \[
        2,4,5,6,7,8,10,11,12,14
    \]
    \item Corresponding $k$ values (primes):
    \[
        1,5,7,11,13,17,19,23,29,31
    \]
    \item First 10 elements of A085302:
    \[
        2,4,5,6,7,8,10,11,12,14
    \]
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Observation:} The sequences match exactly, including the subtle jumps.

\section*{Discussion}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Different definitions:}  
    A085301 counts factorials between primorials; the inequality $n! < n\# + k\#$ defines a minimal additive relationship with a primorial.
    
    \item \textbf{Discrete combinatorial coincidence:}  
    Despite the different constructions, both sequences track factorial growth relative to prime multiplicative structures. The matching of the $n$-sequence appears as a discrete combinatorial ``miracle''.

    \item \textbf{Novelty:}  
    There does not appear to be a known reference connecting the minimal $k$ in $n! < n\# + k\#$ with A085302. This is a remarkable alignment of two independent combinatorial processes.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Conclusion}

The inequality
\[
    n! < n\# + k\#
\]
yields an integer sequence whose indices correspond exactly to A085302. The $k$ values are primes, and their sequence aligns with the cumulative count of factorials below primorials. 

This phenomenon demonstrates a surprising interplay between additive and multiplicative structures of integers, factorials, and primes. A rigorous combinatorial explanation would be highly interesting for further research.
