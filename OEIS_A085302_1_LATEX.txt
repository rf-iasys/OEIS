\begin{abstract}
We explore a previously undocumented connection between factorials, primorials, and prime numbers. Using the inequality
\[
n! < n\# + k\#,
\]
where $n!$ is the factorial of $n$, $n\#$ is the $n$-th primorial, and $k\#$ is the $k$-th primorial, we extract a sequence of $n$ values where the minimal $k$ satisfying the inequality changes. Remarkably, these $n$ values reproduce OEIS sequence A085302 exactly. Furthermore, the corresponding $k$ values are all derived from primes, revealing a natural link between two previously separate number-theoretic sequences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Definitions}

\subsection{Factorials and Primorials}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The factorial of $n$ is
    \[
    n! = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot \dots \cdot n.
    \]
    \item The $m$-th primorial is
    \[
    m\# = \prod_{i=1}^{m} p_i,
    \]
    where $p_i$ is the $i$-th prime number.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Inequality Method}

For each $n$, define
\[
k(n) = \min \{ k \mid n! < n\# + k\# \}.
\]

- $k\#$ is the $k$-th primorial  
- $k(n)$ is the smallest integer for which the inequality holds

We then define $n_{\text{seq}}$ as the sequence of $n$ values for which $k(n)$ increases.

---

\section{Relation to OEIS A085302}

OEIS A085302 defines $a(n)$ as the partial sum of A085301:

\[
a(n)-1 = \# \{ m! \mid m! < n\# \}.
\]

- This counts the number of factorials below the $n$-th primorial.  

\textbf{Observation:} A direct computational comparison shows that

\[
n_{\text{seq}} = a(1), a(2), a(3), \dots
\]

for all computed values (e.g., up to $n_{\max}=5000$), yielding perfect alignment with OEIS sequence A085302.

---

\section{Corresponding Prime-based Sequence}

The $k(n)$ values derived from the inequality are all **prime-indexed via primorials**. That is:

\[
k(n) = \text{position in primes such that } k\# \text{ satisfies the inequality}.
\]

This produces a new sequence (potentially novel) that links factorial growth to primes:

\section{Discussion}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The inequality approach reproduces the OEIS sequence perfectly, for thousands of terms.  
    \item The corresponding $k(n)$ values reveal the underlying prime structure that determines the factorial thresholds.  
    \item Two distinct sequences — additive factorial counts and multiplicative prime growth — are connected through a simple inequality.  
    \item The prime-based $k(n)$ sequence appears to be new and may merit an OEIS submission.
\end{enumerate}

---

\section{Conclusion}

We have demonstrated a **novel duality**:

\[
\text{Factorials under primorials (A085302)} \longleftrightarrow \text{Prime-indexed $k(n)$ via inequality}.
\]

- The method provides both a computational validation and a conceptual insight into the interaction of additive and multiplicative number-theoretic growth.
